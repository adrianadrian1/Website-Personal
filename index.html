English Grammar

Grammar Reference

Learn about different grammar points through our clear and simple explanations.

Verb Tenses

Understand when and how to use different verb tenses in English.

Present Simple

Used for habits, general truths, and permanent situations.

Structure: Subject + base form of verb (add 's' for third person singular)

Examples:

  • I work at a bank.
  • She lives in London.
  • The sun rises in the east.

Present Continuous

Used for actions happening now or around now, and future arrangements.

Structure: Subject + am/is/are + present participle (-ing form)

Examples:

  • I am studying English now.
  • They are playing football at the moment.
  • We are meeting John tomorrow.

Past Simple

Used for completed actions in the past.

Structure: Subject + past form of verb

Examples:

  • I visited Paris last year.
  • She finished her homework an hour ago.
  • They went to the cinema yesterday.

Present Perfect

Used for experiences, changes, and continuing situations.

Structure: Subject + have/has + past participle

Examples:

  • I have seen that movie three times.
  • She has lived here since 2010.
  • They have just arrived.

Modals

Master the use of modal verbs to express ability, permission, obligation and more.

Can, Could, Be Able To

Used to express ability, possibility, permission, and requests.

Examples:

  • I can swim. (ability)
  • Could you help me? (polite request)
  • She was able to finish on time. (past ability)

May, Might

Used to express possibility and permission.

Examples:

  • It may rain tomorrow. (possibility)
  • Might I borrow your pen? (formal permission)
  • We might go to the beach if it's sunny. (uncertain plan)

Must, Have To

Used to express obligation and necessity.

Examples:

  • You must wear a seatbelt. (strong obligation)
  • I have to finish this report today. (external obligation)
  • She must be tired after that long trip. (logical certainty)

Conditionals

Learn how to talk about real and unreal situations using conditional sentences.

Zero Conditional

Used for general truths and facts.

Structure: If + present simple, present simple

Examples:

  • If you heat ice, it melts.
  • If people don't eat, they get hungry.

First Conditional

Used for real and possible situations in the future.

Structure: If + present simple, will + base verb

Examples:

  • If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.
  • If you study hard, you will pass the exam.

Second Conditional

Used for unreal or improbable situations in the present or future.

Structure: If + past simple, would + base verb

Examples:

  • If I won the lottery, I would travel around the world.
  • If I were you, I would apologize.

English Vocabulary

Vocabulary Building

Expand your vocabulary with our themed word lists and usage examples.

Common Topics

Essential vocabulary for everyday situations and conversations.

Work and Jobs

Essential vocabulary for the workplace and career-related discussions.

Key Terms:

  • Resume/CV - A document listing your qualifications and work experience
  • Interview - A formal meeting to assess a job applicant
  • Deadline - The latest time by which something must be done
  • Promotion - Advancement to a higher-level position
  • Colleague - A person you work with

Travel and Tourism

Vocabulary useful for traveling and visiting places.

Key Terms:

  • Boarding pass - Document allowing you to board a plane
  • Itinerary - A planned route or journey
  • Sightseeing - Visiting places of interest
  • Accommodation - A place where travelers can stay
  • Currency exchange - Converting money from one currency to another

Food and Drink

Vocabulary related to eating and drinking.

Key Terms:

  • Appetizer - A small dish before the main course
  • Beverage - A drink
  • Dessert - Sweet food eaten after a meal
  • Ingredients - Items used to prepare a dish
  • Recipe - Instructions for preparing food

Word Types

Different categories of words and how to use them effectively.

Phrasal Verbs

Verbs combined with prepositions or adverbs that create new meanings.

Examples:

  • Give up - To stop doing something
  • Look after - To take care of someone/something
  • Put off - To postpone
  • Take after - To resemble a family member
  • Turn down - To refuse an offer

Idioms

Expressions with meanings that can't be deduced from the individual words.

Examples:

  • Break a leg - Good luck
  • Hit the sack - Go to bed
  • Let the cat out of the bag - Reveal a secret
  • Once in a blue moon - Very rarely
  • Under the weather - Feeling ill

Vocabulary Tips

Effective strategies for learning and remembering new words.

Learn Words in Context

Instead of memorizing isolated words, learn them in sentences or phrases. This helps you understand how they're actually used.

Use Flashcards

Create flashcards with the word on one side and definition/example on the other. Review them regularly using spaced repetition.

Practice Using New Words

Try to use new words in sentences soon after learning them. Writing or speaking practice helps cement them in your memory.

Group Related Words

Learn words in thematic groups (e.g., all kitchen items, all emotions). This creates mental connections that aid recall.

Read Extensively

Reading exposes you to vocabulary in natural contexts. Choose materials at your level and note down new words to look up later.
